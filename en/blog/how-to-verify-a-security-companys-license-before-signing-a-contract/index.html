How to Verify a Security Company's License Before Signing a Contract

Most disputes we hear about between clients and security companies trace back to something that could have been checked before signing: an expired license, guards without individual permits, or a provider operating outside their approved scope.

A Practical Checklist

Ask for the company's MOI license number directly and confirm it's current, not just referenced vaguely in a brochure. For industrial sites, confirm HCIS approval separately, a provider quoting for an oil and gas contract should be able to show this without hesitation.

Ask whether individual guards hold their own personal licenses, not just the company itself, an unlicensed guard working for a licensed company is still a compliance problem for the client hosting them.

Check Nitaqat status where relevant, and ask about field supervision practices, a provider that can't describe how they verify guards are actually at post is one worth asking harder questions of.

Why License Verification Matters More Than It Seems

An unlicensed or improperly licensed security provider isn't just a compliance risk on paper. It means the guards on your site may not have undergone the vetting, training, and background checks the licensing regime is actually designed to ensure. If an incident occurs on your property involving an unlicensed guard, your own business can face liability and reputational consequences well beyond whatever the provider's contract promised.

What to Ask For

Request the company's Ministry of Interior security-guarding license number directly, along with the specific license or certification for each individual guard assigned to your contract: company-level licensing and individual guard licensing are both necessary, and neither substitutes for the other. If your facility falls under HCIS oversight, request separate confirmation of HCIS approval status, since this is a distinct regime from standard MOI licensing.

How to Verify What You're Given

Don't accept a photocopy or a verbal claim as sufficient. Ask how you can independently confirm the license details provided, and be specifically wary of any provider who resists this request or offers only vague reassurances instead of verifiable documentation. A legitimate provider expects this question and has an answer ready.

Vetting Beyond the License Number

Licensing confirms a baseline, but a genuinely diligent provider maintains vetting records beyond the minimum: background checks refreshed on a schedule, training records for each guard, and documentation appropriate to your facility's specific risk category. Ask whether the provider can produce this documentation for review at any point during the contract, not only at signing.

Red Flags During the Verification Process

Be cautious of a provider who takes unusually long to produce licensing documentation, who provides documentation for the company but not for the specific individual guards, or who becomes defensive when asked reasonable verification questions. These behaviors during the pre-contract stage are a preview of how the provider will behave once you're relying on them operationally.

Documenting Verification for Your Own Records

Keep a copy of every license and certification document a provider supplies as part of your own contract file, not just as a one-time check before signing. If a compliance question or incident ever arises during the contract, having this documentation readily available protects your own business's position regardless of how the provider's own records are maintained.

Re-Verification Over Time, Not Just at Signing

Licenses and certifications can lapse or change over a multi-year contract, and periodic re-verification, say, annually, is worth building into your contract's review cycle rather than assuming the documentation you received at signing remains valid indefinitely.

What to Do if You Discover a Problem Mid-Contract

If you discover during an existing contract that a provider's licensing has lapsed or was never fully valid, address it immediately and directly rather than assuming it will resolve itself. This is both a genuine security risk (unlicensed guards may not have undergone proper vetting) and a liability exposure for your own business that gets worse the longer it goes unaddressed.

Verification as an Ongoing Relationship Practice, Not a One-Time Gate

The strongest client-provider relationships treat license and vetting verification as a normal, ongoing part of how the two parties work together: periodic re-checks built into quarterly reviews, transparent updates when a specific guard's certification is renewed or when a new hire joins the contract, and a provider who volunteers this information rather than waiting to be asked. This ongoing transparency is a better predictor of long-term reliability than a single clean verification at the start of the relationship.

A Summary Verification Checklist

Before signing any guarding contract: confirm the company's MOI license number and, if applicable, HCIS approval status directly rather than accepting a copy alone. Request individual guard licensing for the specific personnel assigned to your site. Ask how you can independently verify what's provided. Confirm the provider maintains ongoing vetting documentation beyond the minimum licensing requirement. Build periodic re-verification into your contract review cycle rather than treating the initial check as sufficient for the contract's full duration.

Why This Diligence Pays Off Over Time

Businesses that invest this diligence upfront consistently report fewer disputes, faster incident resolution when something does go wrong, and a stronger negotiating position at contract renewal, since a provider who's been held to a documented standard from day one tends to maintain that standard rather than letting quality quietly slip over an unmonitored relationship.

A Small Investment of Time With Real Long-Term Payoff

Verifying licensing properly before signing takes perhaps an hour of focused attention, a fraction of the time most businesses spend comparing prices across proposals, yet it's the single check most likely to prevent a genuinely serious problem down the road. Treating this verification step as seriously as the price comparison itself is one of the simplest ways to protect your business from a costly mistake.

Making Verification a Habit, Not an Exception

Once you've gone through this verification process for one contract, apply the same discipline to every future guarding relationship, renewal, or expansion, treating license verification as a standard, non-negotiable step in any procurement process, rather than a one-off exercise reserved for a single high-stakes decision, protects your business consistently rather than only occasionally.
